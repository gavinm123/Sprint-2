Overview

Going to university often means managing your own money for the first time.

This page helps you understand:

  • what students typically need to pay for
  • how much those costs can add up to each month
  • how students usually cover their everyday costs
The figures on this page are estimates, based on typical student spending. Your actual costs will depend on where you study, how you live and your circumstances.

If you have never managed your own household costs before, some of these figures may be hard to judge at first. Use this page to understand what your costs are likely to look like, rather than to get an exact figure.

What you'll need to pay for

Understanding the full range of costs can help you plan before you make decisions about where and how to study.

Regular living costs

These are ongoing costs you usually need to pay every month:

  • rent or accommodation (often the biggest cost)
  • food and groceries
  • household bills, such as gas and electricity (if not included in your rent)
  • internet and phone
  • travel and transport
  • toiletries and household basics

One-off and occasional costs

These do not happen every month, but still need planning:

  • textbooks, course materials and equipment
  • a laptop or tablet, if you do not already have one
  • accommodation deposits and setup costs
  • clothing or bedding if you are moving away from home

Costs that are easy to forget

Some costs are not always obvious when you first plan your budget:

  • eye tests and glasses, prescriptions and dentist visits
  • household contents insurance
  • social and leisure costs (these are part of day-to-day life)
  • childcare, if you have dependent children

Some costs come up only a few times a year, but you still need money set aside for them.

How students usually cover their costs

Most students need more money than their maintenance loan alone provides.

Students often cover everyday costs using a combination of:

  • paid work during term time
  • support from family or carers
  • savings or other income

What works depends on your situation.

If your income and costs are close, students often adjust by:

  • working some hours during term time
  • choosing different accommodation
  • budgeting differently day to day

This is normal, especially in your first year.

What support is available

Student finance in England can include different types of support. Not all of it is a loan, and not all of it needs to be paid back.

Loan Tuition Fee Loan

Up to £9,535 per year

Paid directly to your university or college to cover your tuition fees. You don't see this money — it goes straight to your provider. You repay it after you graduate, but only when you earn above the threshold.

About the Tuition Fee Loan >
Loan Maintenance Loan

Up to £13,348 per year

Paid to you in three instalments to help cover living costs. The amount depends on your household income, where you study, and whether you live at home or away. London students receive more.

About the Maintenance Loan >
Grant — does not need to be repaid Disabled Students' Allowance

Support for additional needs

If you have a disability, long-term health condition, mental health condition, or a specific learning difficulty like dyslexia, you may be eligible for additional support.

About Disabled Students' Allowance >
Grant — does not need to be repaid Childcare Grant

Up to 85% of childcare costs

If you have dependent children in registered childcare, you may be able to claim back up to 85% of your childcare costs while studying. This is on top of your student loan and does not need to be repaid.

Grant — does not need to be repaid Parents' Learning Allowance

Up to £1,821 per year

Additional support for student parents to help cover the costs of studying while raising children. Means-tested and paid on top of the maintenance loan.

How repayment works

Student loans in England work differently to other kinds of debt. It's worth understanding this before worries about debt put you off.

You only start repaying when you earn over £25,000 a year (for plan 5), and you repay 9% of whatever you earn.

For example:

  • If you earn under £25,000, you pay £0
  • If you earn £27,000, you pay about £15 a month
  • If you earn £30,000, you pay about £38 a month
  • If you earn £40,000, you pay about £113 a month

Repayments are normally collected through your salary, like tax and National Insurance.

If you haven't repaid the full loan after 40 years, the remaining balance is written off.

This means many graduates — especially those in lower-earning careers — will never repay the full amount.

This is very different from credit card debt or a bank loan, where the full amount is always due.

Your student loan doesn't appear on your credit file in the same way a commercial loan would.

Mortgage lenders will consider your monthly repayments as part of affordability checks, but the loan itself is not treated as standard debt.

Interest is added to your loan, but it works differently to a credit card or bank loans.

The rate is based on the Retail Price Index (RPI) - a measure of inflation - so it rises and falls with the cost of living, rather than being set by a bank.

While you're studying, interest is charged at RPI plus 3%.

Once you've left your course, the rate depends on what you earn:

  • If you earn below the repayment threshold (£25,000), interest is charged at RPI only
  • If you earn above the threshold, interest is charged at RPI plus 3%
  • If you earn somewhere in between, the rate is somewhere between the two

This doesn't change your monthly repayments. You always repay 9% of what you earn above the threshold. The interest rate affects your overall balance, not how much you pay each month

And remember, if the loan isn't fully repaid after 40 years, whatever is left is written off.
