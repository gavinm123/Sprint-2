Overview

Going to university or college costs more than just tuition fees. This page helps you understand what you may need to pay for, what support is available, and how to think realistically about whether you can manage the costs.

The figures on this page are estimates based on typical student costs. Your actual costs will depend on where you study, how you live, and your circumstances. Use these as a starting point, not a guarantee.

What you'll need to pay for

Understanding the full picture of costs helps you plan before you commit to anything. There are three main categories to think about.

Regular living costs

  • Rent or accommodation (usually your biggest cost)
  • Food and groceries
  • Utilities - gas and electricity (if not included in rent)
  • Internet and phone
  • Travel and transport
  • Toiletries and household basics

One-off and occasional costs

  • Textbooks, course materials, and equipment
  • Laptop or tablet (if you don't have one already)
  • Accommodation deposits and setup costs
  • Clothing or bedding if moving away

Costs that are easy to forget

  • GP appointments and perscriptions
  • Dentist visits
  • Household contents insurance
  • Social life and wellbeing - these matter too
  • Childcare, if you have dependent children

What support is available

Student finance in England can include different types of support. Not all of it is a loan, and not all of it needs to be paid back.

Loan Tuition Fee Loan

Up to £9,535 per year

Paid directly to your university or college to cover your tuition fees. You don't see this money — it goes straight to your provider. You repay it after you graduate, but only when you earn above the threshold.

About the Tuition Fee Loan >
Loan Maintenance Loan

Up to £13,348 per year

Paid to you in three instalments to help cover living costs. The amount depends on your household income, where you study, and whether you live at home or away. London students receive more.

About the Maintenance Loan >
Grant — does not need to be repaid Disabled Students' Allowance

Support for additional needs

If you have a disability, long-term health condition, mental health condition, or a specific learning difficulty like dyslexia, you may be eligible for additional support.

About Disabled Students' Allowance >
Grant — does not need to be repaid Childcare Grant

Up to 85% of childcare costs

If you have dependent children in registered childcare, you may be able to claim back up to 85% of your childcare costs while studying. This is on top of your student loan and does not need to be repaid.

About the Childcare Grant >
Grant — does not need to be repaid Parents' Learning Allowance

Up to £1,821 per year

Additional support for student parents to help cover the costs of studying while raising children. Means-tested and paid on top of the maintenance loan.

About Parents' Learning Allowance >
University funding Bursaries and hardship funds

Varies by institution

Many universities and colleges offer their own bursaries, scholarships, and hardship funds, separate from Student Finance England.

How repayment works

Student loans in England work differently to other kinds of debt. It's worth understanding this before worries about debt put you off.

You start making repayments only once your income rises above a threshold — currently £25,000 a year (Plan 5). Below that, you pay nothing, regardless of how much you borrowed.

Repayments are 9% of whatever you earn above the threshold. If you earn £27,000, you repay 9% of £2,000 — that's £180 a year, or £15 a month.

Repayments are normally collected through PAYE, like tax and National Insurance. You do not get a separate bill each month.

If you haven't repaid the full loan after 40 years, the remaining balance is written off. This means many graduates — especially those in lower-earning careers — will never repay the full amount. The loan does not follow you forever.

This is very different from credit card debt or a bank loan, where the full amount is always due.

Your student loan doesn't appear on your credit file in the same way a commercial loan would. Mortgage lenders will consider your monthly repayments as part of affordability assessments, but the loan itself is not treated as standard debt.

Budgeting help

Before you accept a place, it's worth doing a rough budget. You are simply checking whether your likely income covers your likely outgoings.

Income side

  • Maintenance loan
  • Any grants, bursaries, or scholarships
  • Part-time work, if relevant
  • Any family contribution

Costs side

  • Rent or accommodation fees
  • Food, travel, phone, and utilities
  • Course materials and equipment
  • Personal spending
The gap is real. For many students, the maintenance loan does not cover all living costs. Planning ahead for part-time work, family support, or hardship funds is sensible.
